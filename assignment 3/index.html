Marie Antoinette, the Queen of France, was executed by guillotine on October 16, 1793, during the French Revolution.

Her death came after her husband, King Louis XVI, had been executed earlier in January 1793.

This period was part of the Reign of Terror, when revolutionaries, led by the radical Jacobins, sought to eliminate perceived enemies of the Republic.

Marie Antoinette’s execution symbolized the fall of the monarchy and the radical phase of the Revolution.

France then descended deeper into the Reign of Terror (1793–1794).

The Committee of Public Safety, led by Robespierre, intensified its crackdown on anyone suspected of opposing the revolution.

Thousands were executed, including nobles, clergy, and even revolutionaries themselves.

Political instability and fear dominated France until Robespierre’s fall in 1794, which ended the Terror and led to a more moderate government called the Directory.
